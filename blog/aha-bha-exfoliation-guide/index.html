AHA vs BHA: Chemical Exfoliation for Beginners

They're often bundled together on packaging as "acids," but AHA and BHA solve different problems. Here's how to tell which one your skin actually needs.

K Kloré Skin • Updated July 23, 2026 • 6 min read

Quick answer: AHA (like glycolic or lactic acid) works on the skin's surface and is best for dullness, uneven texture, and dry skin. BHA (salicylic acid) is oil-soluble and gets into pores, making it better for blackheads, oily skin, and breakouts. Most people only need one, used 2–3 times a week, not both daily — and it should never be combined with retinol on the same night.

The Actual Difference Between AHA and BHA

AHA — Surface-Level, Water-Soluble

Alpha hydroxy acids like glycolic and lactic acid work on the skin's surface, loosening the bonds between dead skin cells. This helps with dullness, rough texture, and uneven tone, and tends to suit normal-to-dry skin well since it also has a mild hydrating effect.

BHA — Oil-Soluble, Goes Into Pores

Beta hydroxy acid, almost always salicylic acid, is oil-soluble, which lets it get inside pores and clear out the oil and debris that cause blackheads and breakouts. This makes it the better choice for oily or acne-prone skin.

Which One Your Skin Needs

Main Concern Best Choice Why
Dullness, rough texture AHA (glycolic or lactic acid) Smooths the surface layer of skin
Blackheads, clogged pores BHA (salicylic acid) Oil-soluble, clears inside the pore
Oily, acne-prone skin BHA Controls oil and reduces breakouts
Dry or sensitive skin Lactic acid (a milder AHA) Gentler and slightly hydrating

How Often to Exfoliate

More is not better here. Over-exfoliating is one of the most common ways beginners damage their skin barrier without realizing the acid was the cause.

A Reasonable Starting Frequency

  • ✔ 2–3 times a week to start, regardless of AHA or BHA
  • ✔ Always followed by moisturizer
  • ✔ Always followed by sunscreen the next morning
  • ✔ Increase only if skin shows no redness, tightness, or flaking

Fitting It In With Other Actives

This is where most routines go wrong — stacking acids, retinol, and vitamin C into the same week without a plan. A simple way to think about it:

Night Active
Night 1 AHA or BHA
Night 2 Rest — cica, hydration, no actives
Night 3 Retinol
Night 4 Rest — cica, hydration, no actives

Vitamin C is generally kept to mornings, separate from this nightly rotation. If you've read our retinol for beginners guide, this is the same logic — spacing actives out prevents the irritation that makes people quit an ingredient that was actually working.

Common Mistakes

  • Using AHA and BHA in the same routine before your skin has built any tolerance.
  • Combining acid exfoliation with retinol on the same night.
  • Skipping sunscreen the next day — exfoliated skin is more sun-sensitive.
  • Increasing frequency the moment skin feels smoother, instead of waiting a few more weeks.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use AHA and BHA together?

Some formulas combine both at low concentrations, but using two separate high-strength products together increases irritation risk substantially. Most people only need one.

Can I use acids every day?

Most people do better starting at 2–3 times a week and adjusting from there, rather than using acids daily from the start.

Should I exfoliate before or after cleansing?

After cleansing, on dry skin, as part of your treatment step — not as a substitute for cleansing.

Can I use a physical scrub instead?

Chemical exfoliation (AHA/BHA) is generally gentler and more even than physical scrubs, which can cause micro-tears if used too aggressively.

Final Thoughts

The right acid, used a few times a week and given room to work, does more for texture and clarity than layering several actives at once. Pick the one that matches your actual concern, not the one with the most buzz.
