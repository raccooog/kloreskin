Centella Asiatica (Cica): K-Beauty's Fix for Redness & Sensitive Skin

Why almost every Korean skincare shelf has a cica cream on it — and how to use centella asiatica to calm redness, repair your barrier, and get closer to glass skin.

K Kloré Skin • Updated July 18, 2026 • 6 min read

Quick answer: Centella asiatica (often labeled "cica" or "tiger grass") is a plant extract Korean skincare uses to calm redness, speed up barrier repair, and reduce sensitivity. It pairs well with niacinamide and is one of the gentlest actives you can use daily — even on irritated or post-active skin.

What Is Centella Asiatica?

Centella asiatica is a small, leafy plant that's been used in Asian herbal medicine for centuries to help wounds heal. Korean skincare brands extract it into creams, essences, and ampoules — usually labeled "cica," "tiger grass," or "CICA" on the packaging.

Unlike exfoliating acids or brightening actives, cica doesn't change the texture or tone of your skin directly. Instead, it supports the skin's own repair process, which is why it shows up so often in products aimed at redness, sensitivity, and post-treatment recovery.

Why Korean Skincare Relies on Cica

Calms Redness and Irritation

Cica contains compounds (madecassoside and asiaticoside, mainly) that help soothe inflamed or reactive skin. This is why it's the go-to ingredient for skin that's flushed, stinging, or reacting to a new product.

Supports Barrier Repair

A weakened skin barrier lets moisture escape and irritants in. Cica helps rebuild that barrier, which is part of why Korean routines lean on it after over-exfoliating, sun exposure, or switching in a new active too fast.

Speeds Up Recovery After Actives

If you use retinol, acids, or niacinamide, cica is often layered in afterward to reduce the irritation those actives can cause — letting you keep the benefits without the downtime.

Cica vs. Niacinamide — Which One Do You Need?

Both are gentle, both are staples of Korean skincare, and they solve different problems. Most routines eventually use both.

If your main concern is… Reach for Why
Redness, stinging, reactive skin Centella asiatica (cica) Calms and helps repair the barrier
Oily skin, enlarged pores, dullness Niacinamide Regulates oil and brightens tone
Both — sensitive and oily/acne-prone Niacinamide + cica, layered Oil control with a calming buffer

How to Add Cica to Your Routine

Skin Concern Best Format When to Use
Everyday redness Cica toner or essence AM and PM, after cleansing
Post-active irritation Cica cream or balm PM, after your active step
Sunburn or barrier damage Cica gel or sheet mask As needed, until calm
Sensitive skin, long-term Cica moisturizer Daily, as your main moisturizer

Quick Start

  • ✔ Gentle cleanser
  • ✔ Cica toner or essence
  • ✔ Niacinamide serum (if oil/pores are a concern)
  • ✔ Cica moisturizer
  • ✔ Sunscreen

You don't need every cica product on the shelf — one well-chosen step is enough.

Common Cica Mistakes

  • Layering three cica products at once instead of one well-chosen step.
  • Expecting it to brighten or exfoliate — that's not its job.
  • Skipping sunscreen and expecting cica to compensate.
  • Only reaching for it after irritation, instead of using it to prevent it.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use cica every day?

Yes. It's one of the few actives considered safe for daily, long-term use.

Does cica help with acne scars?

It can support the skin's repair process, but it works gradually and isn't a substitute for sun protection.

Can I combine cica with niacinamide?

Yes — this is one of the most common pairings in Korean skincare, especially for sensitive, acne-prone skin.

Is cica good for sensitive skin?

Yes. It's generally considered one of the gentlest actives available, which is why it's often the first ingredient recommended for reactive skin.

Final Thoughts

Cica isn't the ingredient that transforms your skin overnight — it's the one that keeps everything else working without irritation getting in the way. If redness or sensitivity keeps derailing your routine, it's often the missing step, not another active.
